# Day 24: The CHIPS Act & Semiconductor Nationalism — When Silicon Becomes Sovereign

*How a pandemic-fueled chip shortage turned transistors into a matter of national security — and launched a global subsidy arms race that will reshape the industry for decades.*

---

## The Day the World Ran Out of Chips

In early 2021, Ford Motor Company did something it hadn't done in over a century: it parked workers at home because it couldn't build cars. Not because of a steel shortage or a labor strike, but because of a chip that costs about $0.50 — a microcontroller smaller than a fingernail that manages window motors and seat heaters. Ford lost an estimated $3.1 billion in operating profit that year. General Motors, Toyota, Volkswagen — every major automaker faced the same crisis. The global auto industry lost roughly 11.3 million vehicles in 2021, worth over $210 billion in revenue, all because of chips that represent less than 4% of a car's bill of materials.

The chip shortage wasn't just an automotive problem. Sony couldn't build enough PlayStation 5 consoles. Apple delayed its iPhone 13 production. Hospitals struggled to source patient monitors. The U.S. military discovered that some of its weapons systems depended on chips with lead times stretching past 50 weeks.

What had happened was brutally simple in retrospect: decades of efficiency-maximizing globalization had concentrated semiconductor manufacturing in a handful of fabs, mostly in Taiwan and South Korea. When COVID-19 simultaneously spiked demand for laptops and data center capacity while disrupting logistics chains, the system snapped. There was no slack. No strategic reserve. No backup plan.

Suddenly, politicians who had never thought about photolithography in their lives were receiving classified briefings about ASML and EUV scanners. Semiconductors went from being a niche industrial policy topic to the centerpiece of great-power competition overnight.

## The CHIPS and Science Act: America's $280 Billion Bet

On August 9, 2022, President Biden signed the CHIPS and Science Act into law. The headline number — $280 billion — sounds enormous, but the breakdown reveals what Washington actually prioritized. Only $52.7 billion went directly to semiconductor manufacturing and R&D. The remaining $200+ billion authorized (but didn't appropriate) funding for broader science and technology programs at the NSF, DOE, and NIST.

Of that $52.7 billion, the allocations were:

- **$39 billion** in direct manufacturing incentives — grants and loans to build or expand fabs on U.S. soil
- **$13.2 billion** for semiconductor R&D and workforce development
- **$500 million** for international semiconductor supply chain security initiatives
- A **25% investment tax credit** (Section 48D) for semiconductor manufacturing equipment — worth an estimated $24 billion over 10 years

The logic was straightforward but sobering. In 1990, the United States produced 37% of the world's semiconductors. By 2020, that share had cratered to about 12%, and virtually none of it was leading-edge (sub-10nm). The most advanced chips powering America's AI ambitions, F-35 fighter jets, and nuclear weapons simulations were all manufactured on a small island 100 miles off the coast of China.

### The "Guardrails" That Changed Everything

The CHIPS Act's most consequential feature wasn't the money — it was the strings attached. Any company receiving CHIPS Act funding must agree to:

1. **No significant expansion** of leading-edge or advanced semiconductor manufacturing in China or other "countries of concern" for 10 years
2. **Clawback provisions** allowing the government to recoup funds if companies violate these terms
3. **Profit-sharing** mechanisms if a project proves exceptionally profitable
4. **Workforce requirements** including childcare provisions for construction workers (a first for industrial policy)

These guardrails forced a binary choice on chipmakers: take American money and limit your China operations, or stay in China and forgo the subsidies. For companies like Samsung and TSMC, which had significant mainland China fabs, this was a genuinely difficult calculation.

### Where the Money Is Going

The Commerce Department's CHIPS Program Office, led by Under Secretary Laurie Locascio, began awarding grants in early 2024. The major recipients tell the story of American semiconductor strategy:

**Intel:** $8.5 billion in grants plus $11 billion in loans — the largest single award. Intel committed to spending over $100 billion across Arizona, Ohio, New Mexico, and Oregon. The Ohio project alone, near Columbus, will eventually house two leading-edge fabs on a 1,000-acre site. Intel's pitch was simple: it's the only American company that both designs and manufactures leading-edge chips.

**TSMC:** $6.6 billion in grants plus $5 billion in loans for its Arizona complex, where three fabs will produce chips at 4nm (N4), 3nm (N3), and 2nm (A16) nodes. The first fab began limited production in early 2025. TSMC initially struggled with Arizona construction — Taiwanese engineers reportedly clashed with American construction crews over work culture and quality standards. TSMC eventually flew in thousands of experienced workers from Taiwan, sparking a political controversy about H-1B visa usage.

**Samsung:** $6.4 billion for a new fab in Taylor, Texas, targeting advanced nodes including gate-all-around (GAA) transistor production. Samsung's commitment totaled $40+ billion across its Texas operations.

**Micron:** $6.1 billion for memory chip fabs in New York (a new megafab in Syracuse) and Idaho (expansion in Boise), representing over $100 billion in total investment through 2030.

**GlobalFoundries:** $1.5 billion for expanding its Malta, New York fab — notably for trailing-edge chips (12nm and above) used in automotive, defense, and IoT applications. This award acknowledged something important: not every critical chip needs to be cutting-edge.

## The European Chips Act: Brussels Plays Catch-Up

The European Union watched the American CHIPS Act with a mixture of admiration and anxiety. Europe's share of global semiconductor manufacturing had declined from 24% in 2000 to roughly 8% by 2020. The continent had strong positions in automotive chips (Infineon, NXP, STMicroelectronics), equipment (ASML), and materials (Siltronic), but virtually no leading-edge manufacturing.

In September 2023, the European Chips Act entered into force with a target of mobilizing **€43 billion** ($47 billion) in public and private investment, aiming to double Europe's global production share to 20% by 2030.

The Act created three pillars:

1. **Chips for Europe Initiative** (€3.3 billion in EU funds): R&D, pilot lines, design platforms, and workforce training
2. **Supply security framework**: New powers to monitor supply chains and intervene during crises, including the ability to mandate priority-rated orders from fabs during shortages
3. **Investment incentives**: Streamlined state aid approvals for "first-of-a-kind" fab investments

The crown jewel of Europe's strategy was attracting Intel to build a leading-edge fab near Magdeburg, Germany, with €9.9 billion in German government subsidies — the largest industrial subsidy in German history. Intel initially announced the project in 2022, then paused it in late 2024 as part of a broader cost-cutting effort. The fate of the Magdeburg fab became a barometer for Europe's entire semiconductor ambitions.

TSMC, meanwhile, agreed to build a specialty fab in Dresden, Germany — a joint venture called ESMC (European Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) with NXP, Infineon, and Bosch as minority partners. This fab will produce 12nm-28nm chips for automotive applications, not leading-edge logic. It received €5 billion in German subsidies.

### The European Problem

Europe's semiconductor strategy faces a structural challenge: it doesn't have enough customers. The U.S. has hyperscale cloud providers (AWS, Google, Microsoft, Meta) consuming massive volumes of leading-edge chips. China has Huawei, Alibaba, and Baidu. Europe has... nobody buying at that scale. Building a cutting-edge fab without anchor customers is like building an airport without airlines.

The automotive sector — Europe's natural demand driver — predominantly uses chips manufactured at 28nm and above. Only about 2% of automotive chips require sub-10nm processes. Europe arguably needs more trailing-edge capacity, not leading-edge fabs that look impressive in press releases but may struggle to fill order books.

## Japan's Quiet Comeback: Rapidus and the $50 Billion Moonshot

Japan's semiconductor story is one of dramatic fall and attempted resurrection. In 1988, Japanese companies held over 50% of the global semiconductor market. NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi, and Fujitsu were the world's largest chipmakers. By 2020, Japan's share had plummeted below 9%, and no Japanese company manufactured anything close to leading-edge.

Japan's approach to semiconductor nationalism has been the most audacious of all: create a brand-new company to manufacture 2nm chips from scratch.

**Rapidus** (Latin for "fast") was founded in August 2022 by a consortium including Toyota, Sony, NTT, NEC, SoftBank, MUFG, Kioxia, and Denso, with initial corporate investment of ¥7.3 billion ($50 million) — a pittance. The real money comes from the Japanese government, which has committed over **¥3.9 trillion** (roughly $25 billion) to Rapidus and broader semiconductor initiatives.

The plan is extraordinary: Rapidus will license IBM's 2nm gate-all-around nanosheet process technology and build a fab in Chitose, Hokkaido (chosen for its cold climate reducing cooling costs, abundant water supply, and land availability). IBM demonstrated a 2nm test chip at its Albany, New York R&D facility in 2021 but has no manufacturing capability — it sold its fab business to GlobalFoundries in 2015. The partnership gives Rapidus access to IBM's process design kits while IBM gets a potential manufacturing partner for its technology.

The skepticism is warranted. Going from zero to 2nm manufacturing requires mastering EUV lithography, gate-all-around transistor architecture, backside power delivery, and advanced packaging — simultaneously. TSMC spent three decades and hundreds of billions of dollars building that capability incrementally. Rapidus aims to do it by 2027.

The Japanese government is betting that what Rapidus lacks in experience, it can partially compensate for with talent. Japan still has deep expertise in semiconductor equipment (Tokyo Electron is the world's fourth-largest equipment maker) and materials (JSR, Shin-Etsu, SUMCO). The country produces over 90% of the world's photoresists and a similar share of silicon wafers. If any country understands the chemistry and physics of chipmaking at a granular level, it's Japan.

## The Subsidy Arms Race By the Numbers

When you zoom out, the global semiconductor subsidy competition looks like this:

| Country/Region | Announced Semiconductor Subsidies (2022-2025) | Target |
|---|---|---|
| United States | ~$52.7B direct + $24B tax credits | 20% of global leading-edge by 2030 |
| European Union | €43B ($47B) public + private | 20% of global production by 2030 |
| Japan | ¥3.9T+ ($25B+) | Rebuild domestic leading-edge capability |
| South Korea | ₩622T ($472B) tax incentives + subsidies through 2047 | Protect Samsung/SK Hynix dominance |
| China | Est. $150-175B+ (various funds, hard to track) | Self-sufficiency in mature nodes, progress on advanced |
| India | ₹760B ($10B) | Build first major fabs (Tata + PSMC) |
| Taiwan | Tax credits, infrastructure support (smaller scale) | Maintain TSMC's global lead |

South Korea's numbers deserve special attention. The "K-Chips Act" and its associated "Mega Cluster" strategy — a planned 622 trillion won ($472 billion) semiconductor ecosystem near Seoul through 2047 — dwarfs every other national plan. But much of this is projected private investment by Samsung and SK Hynix, incentivized by enhanced tax credits (up to 25% for large companies, 35% for SMEs). South Korea is essentially paying its champions to keep doing what they were already doing, but faster.

## Does It Actually Work?

Here's the uncomfortable question: can you subsidize your way to semiconductor leadership?

Historical evidence is mixed. Taiwan didn't become a semiconductor powerhouse through subsidies alone — it took Morris Chang's vision, TSMC's relentless execution, and decades of process learning. South Korea's memory chip dominance grew from Samsung's willingness to invest through downturns, not primarily from government money. The most successful semiconductor nations built *ecosystems* — clusters of talent, suppliers, customers, and institutional knowledge that compound over time.

On the other hand, China's experience suggests that subsidies without the right ecosystem produce expensive failures. The Tsinghua Unigroup debacle (the company received billions in state funding before defaulting on bonds in 2020), the Wuhan Hongxin scandal (a $20 billion fab project that turned out to be largely fraudulent), and numerous other "zombie" chip projects burned through an estimated $20-30 billion in wasted investment.

The CHIPS Act addresses some of these risks. The Commerce Department's review process has been notably rigorous — companies must submit detailed business plans, demonstrate customer demand, and accept clawback provisions. The 25% investment tax credit (available to any qualifying manufacturer, not just grant recipients) may actually prove more impactful than the grants themselves because it reduces the ongoing cost disadvantage of U.S. manufacturing.

The fundamental cost gap is real. BCG and the Semiconductor Industry Association estimated in 2020 that a new fab costs 25-50% more to build and operate in the United States than in Asia, driven by higher construction labor costs, more expensive utilities, and fewer experienced semiconductor workers. The CHIPS Act subsidies approximately close this gap for the initial construction — but operating costs remain higher, and subsidies don't train the tens of thousands of process engineers, equipment technicians, and yield specialists that a mature semiconductor cluster requires.

## The Deeper Game: Technology Controls

While subsidies grab headlines, the sharper edge of semiconductor nationalism is export controls — and here, the United States has wielded its power more aggressively than in any domain since the Cold War.

Starting in October 2022, the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) imposed sweeping restrictions on China's access to advanced semiconductor technology:

- **Equipment restrictions**: No American company can sell semiconductor manufacturing equipment to China that enables production at or below 14nm FinFET (or equivalent). Applied Materials, Lam Research, and KLA — which together dominate etch, deposition, and inspection — were effectively banned from selling their most advanced tools to Chinese fabs.
- **EDA software controls**: The October 2022 rules and subsequent updates restricted China's access to the design tools needed for gate-all-around transistor development.
- **Talent restrictions**: U.S. persons (citizens and permanent residents) are prohibited from supporting the development or production of advanced semiconductors at Chinese fabs — a "brain drain" provision that forced dozens of American engineers working at Chinese chip companies to resign.

In January 2023, the U.S. persuaded the Netherlands and Japan to impose complementary restrictions. ASML cannot sell EUV lithography systems to China (this was already effectively the case since 2019, but now DUV immersion scanners were also restricted). Tokyo Electron faces similar constraints on its advanced equipment.

These controls represent a fundamental shift from the post-Cold War consensus that technology trade promotes stability. The explicit goal, as national security advisor Jake Sullivan articulated, is to maintain "as large a lead as possible" — not just prevent military applications, but slow China's entire advanced semiconductor ecosystem.

## What's Actually Getting Built

As of early 2026, the concrete results of this subsidy race are beginning to materialize:

- TSMC's first Arizona fab (Fab 21 P1) has begun producing 4nm chips, with Apple reportedly among the first customers
- Intel's Ohio site has broken ground, though the timeline has slipped from the original 2025 production start
- Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab construction continues, targeting production in 2026
- Rapidus's Hokkaido fab has a shell building completed, with tool installation underway
- TSMC's Dresden ESMC fab received approval and began construction
- Micron's New York megafab site preparation is underway

The total capital being deployed is staggering — over $300 billion in committed semiconductor fab investment across the United States, Europe, and Japan from 2022 to 2030. This is the largest industrial policy intervention in the semiconductor industry since its invention.

Whether these fabs will be economically viable without perpetual subsidies remains the trillion-dollar question. A leading-edge fab burns through $5-8 billion in equipment alone and needs to run at 80%+ utilization to be profitable. Finding enough customers to fill all this new capacity — especially in regions where the semiconductor ecosystem is thin — will be the defining challenge of the next decade.
